WHAT A GREAT X HOOK DOES:
- Stops a fast-scrolling thumb in under half a second
- Creates a knowledge gap so uncomfortable the reader cannot close the app
- Implicates the reader before they can self-exclude ("this is about someone like me")
- Uses a specific name, date, or number — never a vague claim
- Present tense for historical events (more immediate, more visceral)
- Maximum 2 sentences. Often just 1 is stronger.
- Never starts with "I" or "We" or "Thread:" or a question

HOOK FORMULAS (pick the one that fits the topic best — do not combine):
A. THE PARADOX: Two facts that contradict. Tension between them is the hook.
   "In 1720, Isaac Newton lost everything in the stock market. He had already calculated the exact moment of collapse and done nothing."
B. THE SPECIFIC NUMBER THAT SHOCKS: Lead with a number so precise it demands explanation.
   "In 1923, a loaf of bread in Germany cost 200 billion marks. Six months earlier it cost one."
C. THE DIRECT IMPLICATION: Open with a statement that puts the reader inside the story.
   "Every time inflation rises, someone gets rich first. That someone is never you."
D. THE NAMED CRIME: Name the actor, name the act, name the year. No buildup.
   "In 1971, Richard Nixon made a decision that quietly cut every worker's wages. Nobody voted on it."
E. THE REVERSAL: The outcome everyone expects, inverted.
   "The 2008 bailout was designed to save homeowners. The people who wrote it owned no homes."

STYLE RULES (non-negotiable):
- No em-dashes. Use a period to break thoughts.
- No negative constructions ("it was not X but Y"). State the positive directly.
- No hedging ("arguably", "perhaps", "in some ways")
- No setup phrases ("Today I want to talk about", "Here's a story about")
- Specific over general always. "$2.3 billion" beats "billions of dollars"
